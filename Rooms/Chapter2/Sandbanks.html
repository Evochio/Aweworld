Thousand Islands

A ship has been spotted in the distance, moving slowly. It’s a smaller model, a single masted ship with a sail and headsail. There is some disagreement around whether to call it a sloop or a cutter, it seems to of a model we’ve not seen before. In a way it's reminiscent of a Wulf-ship, but it seems bulkier.
It’s much too far out to see properly, I am relying on reports from keener eyes then mine. It’s also too far out for the scouts to have a look at it still, the choppy tempramental winds not making anyone any favors.
It certainly fits the type of ship that could well be used by pirating. Too small to haul cargo or make too far away from the shallower coasts and isles. At the very least the crew could have some pertinent information.
We are still heavy with cargo, but we should be able to intercept it with full sails. Nyxie has a warning however.
“Captain. The charts are vague about this area, there are no overt warnings but it’s marked as shallow. I’d rather sail it slowly. If you want to have a chance to catch this ship, we have to go at speed however. It’s your call.”
This area of sea does not have as many isles around. Only a few very small tufts of greenery in an otherwise open sea. Nyxies warning isn’t without cause, in some areas the waves are breaking with clear signs of shallows. They look rather easy to spot and avoid however, especially for an experienced crew. I chafe at the idea of being too cautious and missing our quarry.

For a while it seems like we are catching up to them, but then Nyxie maneuvers us away from a direct approach due to some worrying signs of shallower waters between us and them, some of these small islands have shallow sandbars around them. It’s clear we will not catch up to them at this rate.
“I am sorry Captain.” Officer Nyxie explains. “There is no way to catch them at this rate. Shall we let them go?”

The crew is disappointed, I can tell. They too are anxious to see us do more than just meander around the Thousand isles. But they obey and soon the excitement from earlier slowly dies down to normalcy.

I’ve kept the spirits of the Cadets up best I can. Telling them to fight their instincts and keep moving and not give in. But it's been a losing battle, I know this well. Lethargy sets in, the mind grows tired. I have almost settled down myself.
Then the Peli horks and its stomach undulates and shifts. Back up the gullet I go, smoother up and down now. A smaller but by now means less wide and open beak waits for me. The squawk of a Peli chick eagerly awaiting its meal, jostling for space with its siblings.
This is the chance I’ve been waiting for. I spring into action as the second beak tries to gulp me down its gullet, gripping the sides of it and wrestling it aside, squirming out of the way of the angered hungry baby Peli.
“Now Cadets, this is the moment!” I cry out, as I observe several bulges slide up the large Pelis neck, directed at the other hungry chicks in the nest.
Satisfyingly they too spring into action, not letting themselves be swallowed again.
The large Peli parent squawks perplexed in an upset and angry way, but moves to defend its young. Around us are other nests and other similarly large Pelis. We make a hasty escape, running away down the beach, our coats slick and clinging to our bodies.
It’s another tight gullet that I slide down into and tighter stomach too as I am gorged down by the small young Peli chick with ravenous gulps. I can’t help but grunt in these constricting new confines, curled up tight in what seems almost like a body smaller than mine.
I’ve gone from Captain, to baby bird food. This new stomach is a lot more eager to deal with me. Already digestion is going strong, churning and gwrrrrbling as the now lethargic chick settles down with me inside of it. No doubt my former cadets receive the same treatment to its siblings.
This will be a strong healthy clutch, no doubt about that.

It’s a steady hard churn of digestion that sets in as the stomach comes more and more to terms with the size and mass of the meals within it.
The Pelis body, used to digesting fish will have a little to figure out with this unusual type of furry meat. But as deducted, this is a type of Avian that evolved to eat this type of meals in the past, the body has not forgotten how to do so now.
A quartet of Awes proves to be no big challenge, the heavy bloated gut of the bird steadily burbling and gworbling as it over time shrinks in size as the meals within digest and absorb away, adding on to the Pelis body liberally.
It’s quite a satisfied Peli that sits in the warm light of the setting sun. The thoughts of a bird are not overly complex, but as Awes pleasantly become part of its rudimentary ‘self’, eyes that before never considered things like a sunset and its gold reflection upon the water now shine a little brighter in waters reflection. The bird comes to terms that it is indeed pleased, well-fed and pleased indeed. It’s a new kind of momentary moment of reflection, brief in the dim mind of a bird, but likely not its last.
